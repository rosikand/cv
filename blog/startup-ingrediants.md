# The Ingredients required to Start a Startup

::: {.date}
March 25, 2026
:::


If you want to start a startup: you should only do so when you have the following (in roughly order of importance):

1. **Ability to leverage capital**: be legible to capital and investors to fundraise you. Ideally king make you. 
2. **Ability to acquire customers**: who do you know who can test your product? Are you well networked? Do people know who you are? 
3. **Ability to attract talent**: do people want to work with you? Do you have a desirable reputation to be around? 


and two somewhat optional ones: 

4. **Idea**: surprisingly, this is an optional component since this will evolve, but knowing the market and why you have a competitive edge compared to the person next to know will help. 
5. **Experience**: do you know how to run a company? have you seen it first hand as an early employee? Do you know what to do day-to-day. Do you have the expertise to build the product. Note: considered optional since this can often be learned on the fly (but not always). 

### **Positive example**: 

Robotics researcher graduates from Berkeley, working on robot learning. Joins Tesla as an autopilot researcher. Spends 2 years there (1. legibility to capital). Sees that the team needs high-quality data to succeed. Realizes other players also need this data (2. your first *customers*). Your research you've been developing on world models can act as a source. You leave to start a company to build this out (4. the idea). You know how to build the product (5. experience; albeit you'll have to do it with less resources). Half of the team you worked with at Tesla wants to join you and you give them a few percentage points of equity in return (3. attract talent). You only choose the best two to keep the talent density high. Because you met the 5 points above, and your known in certain networks, your legibility to capital exposes you to high class investors. Tier 1 funds race to set up meetings with you, offering fancy perks like cool helicopter rides. You, as a researcher, have never done anything like this. You take these perks and the best offer. You go back to the drawing board and build out your company. The tier 1 investor yields you a valuation far above your initial goal. You use this capital to sell your first products at a loss and attract talent. The VC gives you your first ten employees (all MIT grads eager to work for you and brand name VC) and lab contacts for customers. 

### **Negative example**: 

Jackpot Johnny graduates from Stanford. Not in the CS department, but with an MS&E degree. He works as an operator at early stage startup for a year doing the in meta thing. He does ok work. Nothing spectacular. Nothing terrible. Another startup is struggling to attract top talent. They recruit you, a recent Stanford grad. Realizes world models are the next big thing. You cold call 10 research labs working on them to see if you can sell them data. You get 3-4 meetings with high-stakes people. You gain a boost of energy from the meetings ("I see the path to riches"). They keep the commitments vague. You go to investors and scrape a few checks from funds who can't deploy into higher status startups. These investors can't get you customers or talent. So your on your own. The initial contacts you got at the labs don't return your calls. You read they went with a competitor, in the news. This gives fuel to the fire and you proceed to grind relentlessly until you either can't anymore, the market is telling you that you have no path to success, and/or you have no cash to run the business. Finally, after 3 years, you call it quits, a few more grey hairs, personal relationships strained, and your back to square one: working as an "operator" at a startup in the next in meta space. The cycle repeats. You're now pushing 30. No one wants to help you anymore and you don't have a partner to come to to eat dinner with. 

*Moral of the story: make sure you have these points satisfied before you "start a startup".*
